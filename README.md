# CS255-Portfolio-Submission
DriverPass Project with Business Requirements Document and System Design Document

DriverPass was a project for a fictional driving school whose owners, Liam and Ian, wanted to move their business online. They needed a cloud-based system that would let students create accounts, purchase training packages, schedule driving lessons, and access online practice tests and course material. On the administrative side, they needed tools for secretaries to manage reservations and customer records, and for the administrator to control user access, manage packages and vehicles, and pull activity reports. The business requirements document captured all of that from the interview transcript, and the system design document translated those requirements into UML diagrams covering use cases, activity flows, sequencing, and class structure.

What I think I did well was staying grounded in what the client actually said rather than designing for what seemed technically interesting. The diagrams reflect specific things Liam and Ian asked for, like tracking who modified each reservation, supporting both online and phone-based booking, and enforcing different access levels by role. If I were going back to revise one thing, it would be the class diagram. The relationships between classes could be more precisely notated, particularly the multiplicity labels, and a Payment class is missing even though payment processing was part of the requirements. Interpreting user needs well means resisting the urge to over-engineer and instead asking what problem the user is actually trying to solve. Liam was not asking for a sophisticated platform for its own sake; he was trying to stop losing business to competitors with better online access. Keeping that goal in mind shaped every design decision. Going forward I would continue starting with requirements elicitation before touching any diagrams, using the client's own words to validate that the design addresses real needs rather than assumed ones, and building iteratively so there is room to adjust when something does not hold up under review.
